Stoic & Stoic‑Spirited Timeline

A chronological walk through rulers, sages, activists, and mystics who embodied resilience, virtue, and reason—long before and after the formal Stoic school of Athens.

c. 2400 BCE — Egypt

Vizier Ptahhotep

Authored the Maxims: moderation, justice, and calm self‑control.

599–527 BCE — India

Mahavira

Jain teacher of total non‑attachment and fearless ascetic endurance.

563–483 BCE — India

Siddhartha Gautama (“The Buddha”)

Sought freedom from craving; taught the Middle Way of equanimity.

551–479 BCE — China

Confucius

Virtue of ren, duty, and humility despite political setbacks.

6th c BCE — China

Laozi

Taught wu wei—effortless alignment with the way of nature.

c. 400 BCE — China

Zhuangzi

Parables of radical freedom from fear, fame, and fixed identity.

470–399 BCE — Greece

Socrates

Unflinching search for virtue; drank the hemlock in serene resolve.

412–323 BCE — Greece

Diogenes of Sinope

Radical Cynic poverty: “I have nothing yet lack nothing.”

350–280 BCE — Greece

Hipparchia of Maroneia

Noblewoman who chose Cynic hardship and fearless public debate.

334–262 BCE — Cyprus → Athens

Zeno of Citium

Founded Stoicism; preached living according to reasoned nature.

330–230 BCE — Asia Minor

Cleanthes

Water‑carrier by night, Stoic hymnist by day—labour & fortitude.

304–232 BCE — India

Ashoka the Great ⚖︎

Converted from conquest to dharma & welfare; edicts on non‑violence.

95–46 BCE — Rome

Cato the Younger ⚖︎

Austere senator who chose death over compromise of liberty.

c. 73–42 BCE — Rome

Porcia Catonis

Stoic fortitude symbol—wounded herself to prove secret‑keeping grit.

4 BCE–65 CE — Hispania/Rome

Seneca the Younger

Essays on anger & tranquility; met forced suicide calmly.

4 BCE–30 CE — Judea

Jesus of Nazareth

Forgiveness under torture; “treasures in heaven, not on earth.”

c. 50–135 CE — Phrygia/Greece

Epictetus

Former slave: “We control only our judgments.”

86–161 CE — Rome

Antoninus Pius ⚖︎

23 years of steady, humane governance; no showy wars.

121–180 CE — Rome

Marcus Aurelius ⚖︎

Wrote the Meditations amid war & plague—duty, humility, reason.

c. 360–415 — Alexandria

Hypatia

Scholar killed for calm, rational teaching amid turmoil.

369–286 BCE — China

Zhuangzi

Detachment parables: butterfly dream, fish in a jar.

fl. 5th–6th c — India → China

Bodhidharma

Nine‑year cave meditation—founder of Zen’s wall‑gazing.

480–524 — Italy

Boethius

Consolation of Philosophy written on death row.

570–632 — Arabia

Muhammad

Endured persecution; ruled humbly; forgave conquerors of Mecca.

8th c — India

Shantideva

Bodhicaryāvatāra on boundless patience & compassion.

501–579 — Persia

Khosrow I ⚖︎

Nicknamed “the Just”; hosted exiled Greek philosophers.

714–801 — Iraq

Rabiʿa al‑ʿAdawiyya

Ascetic Sufi of selfless divine love; torch & water parable.

598–649 — China

Emperor Taizong ⚖︎

Merit exams, open trade, candid advisors—Golden Age start.

763–809 — Baghdad

Harun al‑Rashid ⚖︎

Funded House of Wisdom, translation movement, arts & science.

849–899 — England

Alfred the Great ⚖︎

Rebuilt learning & law after Viking wars; translated philosophy.

1137–1193 — Middle East

Saladin ⚖︎

Merciful capture of Jerusalem; died almost penniless from charity.

1181–1226 — Italy

Francis of Assisi

Joyful poverty; brotherhood with all creatures.

1217–1255 — Mali

Sundiata Keita ⚖︎

From crippled exile to charter of human rights (Kurukan Fuga).

1332–1406 — Tunisia

Ibn Khaldun

Cyclical history & resilience of asabiyyah.

1402–1472 — Texcoco

Nezahualcóyotl ⚖︎

Poet‑king; humane laws & ban on sacrifice in his capital.

1397–1450 — Korea

Sejong the Great ⚖︎

Created Hangul; championed science & fair taxes.

1478–1535 — England

Thomas More

Chose conscience over king; faced execution with wit.

1522–1591 — Japan

Sen no Rikyū

Zen tea master of wabi‑sabi simplicity & death dignity.

1543–1616 — Japan

Tokugawa Ieyasu ⚖︎

Patient unifier: “Life is like holding snow—do not rush.”

1582–1663 — Angola

Queen Nzinga ⚖︎

Fierce resistance to colonial Portugal; later ascetic court.

1584–1645 — Japan

Miyamoto Musashi

Samurai strategist; cave hermit writing Five Rings.

1745–1797 — Nigeria → Britain

Olaudah Equiano

Abolitionist memoirist; bought freedom, argued with reason.

1818–1895 — USA

Frederick Douglass

From slavery to eloquent advocate of liberty & dignity.

c. 1822–1913 — USA

Harriet Tubman

Risked life leading 70+ people to freedom—iron resolve.

1840–1904 — Nez Perce

Chief Joseph

“I will fight no more forever” — dignified surrender.

1905–1997 — Austria

Viktor Frankl

Man’s Search for Meaning: freedom in choosing attitude.

1906–1945 — Germany

Dietrich Bonhoeffer

Moral resistance to Nazism; serene before execution.

1869–1948 — India

Mahatma Gandhi

Non‑violent satyagraha; ascetic life & hunger strikes.

1918–2013 — South Africa

Nelson Mandela

27 years prison; emerged forgiving, unified a nation.
