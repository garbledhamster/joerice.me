Experience

I've played video games my whole life, especially growing up as the youngest of three with the closest sibling gap being five years. That meant I got tossed into the deep end of competitive stuff like 007 GoldenEye on the N64, or I'd be 100%-ing Zelda: Ocarina of Time and Majora’s Mask by racing to the local library to print out guides.

I got my first computer when I was 11, after my dad reconnected with a high school buddy who was finishing up at Gateway Technical College in Elkhorn, Wisconsin. That's when I dove into coding, built Counter-Strike maps, messed with keygens, and learned a ton about the Windows registry. Basically, I was a black hat wannabe at my core, just soaking in everything I could.

Fast forward to my sophomore year of high school in Odenville, Alabama, where the school literally employed me as a "techy." I’d get full credits each semester just for doing IT work. That felt like hitting the jackpot—real-world experience while still a teenager.

Then I ended up graduating high school in Wisconsin, and it was kind of surreal walking the same halls I’d started in as a freshman, only now feeling way older and seeing everyone I knew just a couple of years further along than they were before.

After high school, I ended up going to Gateway Technical College myself and got a degree as a Support Specialist, which was a natural next step after all those years tinkering with computers. And now I'm also a certified network and security specialist—it keeps me on my toes, constantly learning and staying ahead of the game.

I worked at the post office for about 2.5 years—originally, I was supposed to just be part-time, but the clerk running the office got sick with cancer, so I ended up managing that entire operation six days a week for two years. After that, I was “promoted” to the main office, which I loathed with a passion. Deep down, I knew that wasn’t my destiny. I was still going to school in the evenings, and that’s when a friend introduced me to this "MSP" world I’d never heard of. I decided to take a shot, and that’s where my journey into the capitalist tech realm kicked off.

I moved up quickly—residential role, then tier 1, then onto the projects team in a position my coworkers affectionately called "projects bitch." I got to work under two engineers with a combined 50 years of experience, and for around four of those years, I was directly shadowing one of the owners like a total sponge. His patience in teaching me everything shaped how I think today, pushing me to be pretty decent forward-thinker and problem-solver.

Now, I’m n lead engineer at that same MSP where. My day-to-day revolves around coding, design, planning, and execution of all kinds of systems. I’ve got a wide range of responsibilities because, honestly, what I do can’t be pinned down with a neat little label. It's become a place of steady growth for me, and that’s exactly how I like it.
