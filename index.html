As you might have guessed, I’m a parent, a self-learner, and a problem-solving enthusiast. I’m also what the author Lindsay C. Gibson might call an “internalizer,” which is a fancy way of saying I tend to do a lot of the emotional lifting in my relationships. Not gonna lie—it can be tiring, but it does help me see the best in people (most days).

I’m far from perfect, but I like to think our imperfections are what make us interesting—and probably a little funnier, too.

Experience

My love for tech started early, thanks to being the youngest of three and usually the guinea pig when it came to gaming. I cut my teeth on 007 GoldenEye for N64 (yes, I got whooped plenty). I’d also bike to the local library to print out game guides for Zelda: Ocarina of Time and Majora’s Mask—my idea of high adventure back then.

When I was 11, my dad’s friend from Gateway Technical College gave me my first computer, launching me straight into coding, Windows registry diving, and, well, random hacking experiments that made me feel like I was in a secret spy movie. By high school, I was basically “the IT kid,” earning full credits while fixing the school’s computers.

I eventually finished high school back in Wisconsin—same place I started as a freshman, but it felt worlds apart. After that, I followed my dad’s buddy’s footsteps to Gateway Technical College, snagged a Support Specialist degree, and later layered on network and security certifications. Yep, I’m the proud geek who found actual joy in learning about subnets and firewalls.

I briefly worked at the post office, which sounds cozy, but in reality, I ended up running the place six days a week for two years when a coworker fell ill. After a less-than-pleasant transfer to the main office, I knew it was time to move on. A friend nudged me toward an MSP (Managed Service Provider), a world I never knew existed, and I jumped in headfirst.

I started at the bottom, moved to tier 1, joined the projects team, and then shadowed one of the owners for about four years. That’s when I really learned to problem-solve and think ahead. Now, I’m a lead engineer at the same MSP, wearing many hats: coding, designing systems, planning out migrations, and orchestrating just about anything else that needs to happen. It’s a wild ride, and I wouldn’t have it any other way.

Purpose

This site is my corner of the internet—a place to share my projects, experiences, and hopefully a few laughs. It’s not just a portfolio for potential employers; it’s also a public scrapbook of where I’ve been and where I’m headed. I value honesty, growth, and community, so you’ll find bits of all three sprinkled throughout. Whether you’re here to check out my tech work or to spy on my reading list, I hope you find something that resonates.

Support

Your support helps me cover the bills for hosting, domains, and email services. In other words, it keeps these sites from crashing and burning at the worst possible time. You can chip in via my Buy Me a Coffee page—basically fueling my caffeine habit for the greater good.

In practical terms, here’s what your contributions go toward:

  • Domain Fees: So I can keep friendly URLs alive instead of relying on complicated IP addresses.
  • GitHub Pages Fees: Hosting that’s stable (most of the time).
  • Email Fees: Because reliable email is still more professional than carrier pigeons.
  • Email Security Fees: Helping me lock down email so nobody gets spammed with “you won a million bucks!” messages.
  • Cloudflare: For DNS management and some extra muscle on the security front.
  • Time/Materials: Encourages me to keep building new stuff, instead of binge-watching TV shows all weekend (though that’s tempting).
  • Family Support: My wife and I appreciate every bit of stability we can get—especially with kiddos around.

Even a small gesture goes a long way. Thank you for being part of this ongoing adventure!
